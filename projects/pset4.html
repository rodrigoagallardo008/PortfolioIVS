1. Data Context

The dataset used in this project comes from the Guttmacher Institute and contains state-level information about abortion services in the United States. The dataset includes measures such as abortion rates, number of providers, clinic counts, and indicators of geographic access to abortion services.

For this first visualization, I focus specifically on the variable “Percent of counties without a known abortion clinic (2020)”. This measure highlights geographic barriers to abortion access by showing how much of each state lacks a clinic provider. Rather than focusing on the number of abortions performed, this metric emphasizes how unevenly abortion services are distributed across space.

  • Unit of analysis: U.S. state
  • Primary measure: Percent of counties without a known abortion clinic (2020)
  • Geographic scope: United States
  • Time period: 2020

2. Proposition

Proposition: Access to abortion services in the United States is declining.

The next two visualizations use the same dataset but present opposing arguments. This first visualization is intentionally designed to persuade the reader that abortion access is becoming more limited by emphasizing geographic barriers to clinics.

3. Visualization A — Argues for the proposition

This visualization is designed to persuade the reader that abortion access in the United States is declining by emphasizing the large share of counties in many states that do not have a known abortion clinic. The chart focuses on geographic absence rather than service totals, encouraging the viewer to think about access as a question of local availability and physical proximity.

States are sorted from highest to lowest percentage of counties without clinics, which immediately foregrounds the most extreme cases. By filtering the chart to show only states where more than 70% of counties lack a clinic, the visualization concentrates attention on the strongest evidence for a narrative of shrinking access. Together, these choices frame the dataset as showing widespread geographic barriers to abortion care.

3A. Key design decisions

Decision 1: Filtering the dataset to states where more than 70% of counties lack a clinic Score: -1.5

I filtered the dataset so that the chart only includes states where at least 70% of counties lacked a known abortion clinic. This choice makes the overall problem appear more severe by excluding states with lower levels of geographic inaccessibility. While the underlying values are accurate, this restricted frame narrows the viewer’s perspective and strengthens the impression that poor access is the dominant national pattern.

Decision 2: Sorting states from highest to lowest percentage of counties without clinics Score: 1

The bars are ordered from the highest percentage of counties without clinics to the lowest, placing the most extreme cases at the top. This makes the chart easier to read and compare, but it also reinforces the intended argument by ensuring that viewers encounter the strongest evidence first. I considered leaving the states in alphabetical order, but that would have weakened the persuasive impact and made the pattern less immediately visible.

Decision 3: Using a red gradient color scale to encode severity Score: -0.5

I used a red gradient so that darker bars correspond to higher percentages of counties without clinics. Although color is a standard encoding for magnitude, the choice of red also carries emotional associations with urgency, danger, and alarm. This subtly amplifies the seriousness of the access problem and helps the chart feel more forceful than a more neutral palette would have.

Decision 4: Framing the chart with a strongly interpretive title and annotations Score: -1

The title and annotations guide the viewer toward interpreting the chart as evidence of declining abortion access. Phrases such as “severe geographic barriers to access” and “near-total clinic absence across counties” focus attention on the most extreme values and encourage the reader to treat them as representative of a broader national trend. These textual elements do important rhetorical work by shaping not just what the viewer sees, but how the viewer is meant to interpret it.

Decision 5: Measuring access through county-level clinic absence rather than abortion counts Score: -0.5

I chose to visualize the percentage of counties without a clinic rather than the total number of abortions or abortion rates. This measure supports the proposition more effectively because it frames abortion access in terms of physical availability and spatial barriers instead of service volume. I considered using total abortion counts, but that would have made it easier to argue that services remain active and available, which would weaken the intended message of decline.

4. Visualization B — Argues against the proposition

This visualization presents an alternative interpretation of the same dataset, arguing that abortion access in the United States may not necessarily be declining. Instead of focusing on the geographic distribution of clinics, this chart emphasizes the abortion rate per 1,000 women of reproductive age across U.S. states. By shifting the metric from clinic availability to service utilization, the visualization highlights where abortion services are still actively used.

When viewed through this lens, many states continue to show relatively high abortion rates. Several states—including New York, New Jersey, Maryland, and the District of Columbia—display particularly high service rates, suggesting that abortion services remain widely utilized in certain regions. This framing encourages the viewer to interpret the data as evidence that abortion services are still accessible and actively used across the country despite uneven geographic distribution of clinics.

4A. Key design decisions

Decision 1: Using abortion rates instead of clinic distribution Score: -1

Rather than focusing on the geographic absence of clinics, this visualization uses abortion rates per 1,000 women as the primary metric. This framing shifts the viewer’s attention away from spatial access barriers and toward the continued use of abortion services. Because the dataset includes both measures, selecting abortion rates supports a narrative in which abortion services appear active and ongoing rather than restricted.

Decision 2: Including all states without filtering Score: 1.5

Unlike the previous visualization, which filtered the dataset to show only states with the highest percentages of counties without clinics, this chart includes all states. Showing the complete dataset provides a broader view of abortion service rates and allows readers to see that services occur across much of the country. This decision makes the visualization appear more balanced and comprehensive.

Decision 3: Using a neutral blue color palette Score: 1

A blue color palette is used instead of the red palette used in the previous chart. Blue tends to communicate neutrality and stability in many data visualizations. This choice softens the emotional tone of the graphic and subtly reinforces the interpretation that abortion services remain present and stable rather than declining.

Decision 4: Title framing emphasizing continued service presence Score: -0.5

The chart title frames the visualization as evidence that abortion services remain substantial across the United States. Titles strongly shape how readers interpret visualizations, and this phrasing directs the viewer toward focusing on the continued presence of abortion services rather than potential access barriers.

5. Final Reflection

Developing these two visualizations revealed how strongly design choices can shape the interpretation of the same dataset. During the exploratory phase, I noticed that the dataset contained both measures of geographic access to abortion clinics and measures of abortion service usage. By choosing which variable to highlight, it became possible to construct two very different narratives from the same information. Small design decisions such as filtering the data, sorting states, and selecting color palettes had a large impact on how the patterns were perceived. In particular, filtering the first chart to only include states where more than 70% of counties lacked a clinic made the problem appear much more widespread than if the full dataset were shown.

This exercise illustrates that ethical data visualization is not simply about presenting accurate numbers, but also about how those numbers are framed and contextualized. Many persuasive techniques—such as selective filtering, framing titles, and emphasizing particular metrics—can remain technically truthful while still shaping the viewer’s interpretation in subtle ways. Because of this, the boundary between persuasive and misleading visualization is often difficult to define. In practice, ethical visualization should aim to make the viewer aware of the full context of the data and avoid framing choices that systematically hide important information. This assignment demonstrates how easily visual rhetoric can influence understanding, even when the underlying data does not change.

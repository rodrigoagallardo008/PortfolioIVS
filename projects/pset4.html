Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Rodrigo Gallardo — ragallar@mit.edu

1. Data Context

The dataset comes from the Guttmacher Institute and contains state-level information about abortion services in the United States, including clinic counts, abortion rates, provider numbers, and indicators of geographic access.

  • Unit of analysis: U.S. state (50 states + D.C.)
  • Key measures used: % of counties without a known abortion clinic (2020); abortion rate per 1,000 women aged 15-19 by state of residence (2017); % of residents who traveled out of state for care (2020)
  • Time period: Primarily 2017 and 2020 cross-sections, not a continuous time series

Important limitation: Both visualizations use single-year cross-sectional snapshots. No direct trend over time can be shown from this dataset alone.

2. Proposition

Proposition: Abortion access in the United States is severely and unequally distributed: most counties across many states lack any abortion clinic, while a small number of states concentrate the bulk of abortion services.

The two visualizations use the same dataset but emphasize different variables and framings to argue opposing conclusions about how accessible abortion services are in the U.S.

3. Visualization A -- Argues for the proposition

This visualization shows the share of counties in each state that have no known abortion clinic, displayed as a choropleth map. The geographic framing is intentional: the argument is fundamentally spatial, and showing it on a map makes the scale of the problem feel immediate in a way a bar chart does not. Most of the country turns up deep red, and that visual impression does a lot of the persuasive work before the reader even checks the legend.

3A. Key design decisions

Decision 1: Choropleth map instead of bar chart Score: +1

I started with a bar chart and it felt flat. The argument is about geography, and a map lets you actually see that the problem is spread across a huge portion of the country, not just concentrated in a few states. The downside is that low-population states like Wyoming get the same visual weight as Texas, which is a bit misleading, but I think the overall impression the map creates is more honest to the geographic reality than a ranked list would be.

Decision 2: Red color scale anchored at 20%, not 0% Score: -1

The scale starts at 20% instead of 0%, which means states like California (38%) show up as noticeably colored rather than nearly white. If I had used a 0-100% scale, the variation across states would look more gradual and California would look almost fine. Anchoring at 20% makes even the better-performing states look alarming. That is intentionally deceptive, and I kept it because it made the map more persuasive.

Decision 3: Measuring clinic absence rather than abortion rates Score: -0.5

I chose to show whether counties have a clinic at all rather than how many abortions happen, because the clinic metric makes the problem look structural. If I had used abortion rates, several high-absence states would still show decent numbers because residents travel out of state, and that would undercut the argument. The county-level absence measure closes that interpretive gap, though it also means the chart cannot distinguish between a state where no one seeks abortions and one where they just have to travel far to get them.

Decision 4: Hover tooltip includes out-of-state travel data Score: +1.5

The hover tooltip shows the percentage of women living without clinic access and the share who traveled out of state for care, not just the county absence number. I included the travel data even though it partially supports the other side, because hiding it felt like the more obviously deceptive choice. It also actually reinforces the for-argument when you look at it: states with 90%+ county absence tend to have 30-50% out-of-state travel rates, which is itself a sign that in-state access is broken.

Decision 5: Annotating Texas and New York specifically Score: -0.5

Texas is the obvious choice because it is the most populous state with near-total county absence (96%), so it is the strongest single example for the argument. I annotated New York specifically to preempt the "what about blue states" objection. Even New York has 62% county absence, and framing that as "relatively better, but still majority" is a bit slanted since 62% is genuinely much better than 96%. But I wanted to make sure the map did not look like it was only making a point about red states.

4. Visualization B -- Argues against the proposition

This visualization uses a scatter plot to put the two key variables in direct tension: clinic absence on the x-axis and actual abortion rates on the y-axis. If the proposition were fully true, you would expect a strong downward slope, meaning states with almost no clinics would also have very low abortion rates. The scatter shows that relationship is a lot weaker than the map implies, which is the core of the against-argument.

4A. Key design decisions

Decision 1: Scatter plot of clinic absence vs. abortion rate Score: -1

The scatter is the main deceptive move here. By showing that clinic absence and abortion rates are not tightly correlated, I am implying that lack of clinics does not prevent abortions from happening. But a high abortion rate in a high-absence state mostly means residents are traveling to get care, not that access is fine. The chart makes travel look like evidence of access when it is really evidence of a barrier. I went with this encoding anyway because it was the most effective way to visually undercut the map's argument.

Decision 2: Color encodes out-of-state travel, not the same variable as the axes Score: +1

In my earlier version of this chart, color just repeated the bar length, which the grader flagged as redundant and confusing. Here color shows out-of-state travel percentage, which is a genuinely separate dimension. It also makes the chart more analytically interesting: you can see that darker points (high travel) cluster at moderate abortion rates despite high clinic absence, which helps explain the pattern without hiding the mechanism behind it.

Decision 3: Including all states without filtering Score: +1.5

Everything is included here, including Missouri, which is basically the clearest counter-example to the against-argument: its last clinic closed in 2019 and its abortion rate dropped to near zero. I kept it because removing it would have been the more deceptive choice, and because one data point in the lower right corner is easier to explain away as an anomaly than to justify hiding. Showing all states also signals transparency compared to the filtered map in Visualization A.

Decision 4: Annotating Mississippi and Missouri as contrasts Score: -0.5

Mississippi is annotated because it is the strongest single data point for the against-argument: 99% county absence but still a non-trivial abortion rate. Missouri is annotated as the honest counterexample. The framing is slightly slanted because I describe Missouri as a singular case rather than a warning about what the whole map could look like if clinic closures continue, but I think including both makes the chart more credible than if I had only shown the favorable examples.

Decision 5: Title "Abortion Rates Persist Even Where Clinics Are Scarce" Score: -0.5

The word "persist" does a lot of work. It implies resilience, like services are holding on despite obstacles, rather than something like "people are forced to travel further to get care." I tried a more neutral title like "Clinic Scarcity and Abortion Rates by State" and it just did not push the reader toward any conclusion at all, which defeated the point of the exercise. The current title is editorially slanted but not so obviously that it would immediately tip off a skeptical reader.

5. Final Reflection

The thing that surprised me most was how much the choice of metric alone determined the conclusion, before any design decision about color or sorting or filtering even came into play. Visualization A asks whether your county has a clinic. Visualization B asks whether people in your state are still obtaining abortions. Both questions are answered honestly by the data, but they lead to completely opposite takeaways. In Visualization B, out-of-state travel shows up as a non-zero abortion rate and looks like evidence that access is fine, when it is actually evidence that people are facing real barriers. That felt like the most uncomfortable thing I did in this assignment, because the numbers themselves are not wrong, but the interpretation the chart encourages is.

I also came away with a much more concrete sense of what makes color encoding honest or not. In my first version of these charts, color just repeated whatever the bar length was already showing, which added emotional weight but no real information. Giving color a distinct role, like out-of-state travel in Visualization B, makes the chart feel more trustworthy and also more analytically useful. I think the line between persuasion and deception comes down to whether a careful reader could figure out what you left out. Sorting and filtering that are mentioned in the caption are persuasive. Metrics chosen specifically to close off a counter-reading, or color scales anchored to make moderate values look alarming, start crossing into deception because they work on readers who are not already suspicious.

6. Data Transformations & Notes

  • Visualization A: All 50 states included (D.C. excluded, no county-level geography for choropleth). Variable: % of counties without a known abortion clinic (2020).
  • Visualization B: All 50 states + D.C. included. X: % of counties without a clinic (2020). Y: abortion rate per 1,000 women aged 15-19 (2017). Color: % who traveled out of state (2020).
  • Known limitation: Clinic absence (2020) and abortion rate (2017) are from different years, so direct comparison within each scatter point is imprecise.
  • No derived fields: All variables taken directly from the Guttmacher dataset without additional calculation.

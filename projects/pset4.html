Assignment 4 Persuasive or Deceptive Visualization?

Rodrigo Gallardo — ragallar@mit.edu

1. Data Context

The dataset used in this project comes from the Guttmacher Institute and contains state-level information about abortion services in the United States. It includes measures such as abortion rates, number of providers, clinic counts, and indicators of geographic access to abortion services.

  • Unit of analysis: U.S. state
  • Primary measures: Percent of counties without a known abortion clinic (2020); abortion rate per 1,000 women aged 15–19 by state of residence (2017)
  • Geographic scope: United States (all 50 states + D.C.)
  • Time period: 2017 and 2020 (single cross-sections, not a time series)

Important limitation: Both visualizations use single-year cross-sectional snapshots. Neither dataset includes multiple time points, so no direct "trend over time" can be shown. The proposition and takeaway messages are framed accordingly.

2. Proposition

Proposition: Abortion access in the United States is severely and unequally distributed — most counties across many states lack any abortion clinic, while a small number of states concentrate the bulk of abortion services.

The two visualizations use the same underlying dataset but emphasize different variables and framings to argue opposing conclusions about how accessible abortion services are in the U.S.

3. Visualization A — Argues for the proposition

This visualization argues that abortion access in the United States is severely and unequally distributed by highlighting the share of counties in each state that lack any known abortion clinic. Rather than measuring how many abortions occur, this chart measures physical proximity to care — whether a county even has a provider. By filtering to states where over 70% of counties have no clinic and sorting from worst to best, the chart foregrounds the scale of geographic absence and frames it as the dominant national pattern.

The bars all cluster between 75% and 100%, which intentionally compresses the visible variation to make even the "better" states in this filtered view still appear to have high rates of clinic absence. Color encodes whether a state is in the South or not — a dimension beyond bar length — to highlight that the pattern is not purely regional: non-Southern states appear throughout the chart at similarly extreme levels.

3A. Key design decisions

Decision 1: Filtering to states where more than 70% of counties lack a clinic Score: −1.5

By excluding states with lower percentages of county-level clinic absence, the chart creates the impression that near-total geographic inaccessibility is a dominant national pattern rather than a regional one. States like California, New York, and Connecticut — where clinic distribution is much broader — are entirely absent from the frame. The underlying values are accurate, but the restricted view makes the problem appear more uniform and severe than a full dataset view would. I considered showing all states but found that including states with low clinic-absence rates visually weakened the intended argument.

Decision 2: Sorting states from highest to lowest percentage Score: 1

Sorting places the most extreme cases at the top, making the pattern immediately readable and ensuring viewers encounter the strongest evidence first. This is a standard chart design choice that also serves a persuasive purpose: it frames the worst-case states as the norm rather than the exception. Alphabetical ordering would have obscured the severity of the pattern and made the chart harder to read.

Decision 3: Color encodes region (Southern vs. non-Southern), not the same variable as bar length Score: 0.5

Color here encodes a categorical dimension — whether the state is in the South — rather than repeating the percentage already shown by bar length. This adds genuine information: it counters the possible objection that the chart only reflects a Southern phenomenon, because non-Southern states appear throughout the distribution at similarly high levels. The red hue family was chosen to maintain a sense of severity across both categories, but the two-tone distinction between dark and lighter red makes the regional dimension readable without requiring a separate axis.

Decision 4: Annotations anchored to specific bars at each end of the distribution −0.5

The annotation "Near-total clinic absence (99%)" is placed directly on the Arkansas bar, the highest value in the chart. The annotation "Even at 75%, three in four counties lack any clinic" is placed on the Oregon bar, the lowest value. Together these anchor the reader's interpretation at both ends: even the least extreme state in this filtered view still shows severe absence. The annotations describe the data rather than just labeling it, which adds persuasive force without fabricating any information. The downside is that anchoring at the extremes implicitly discourages the reader from thinking about what was excluded.

Decision 5: Measuring clinic absence rather than abortion counts or rates Score: −0.5

Focusing on the percentage of counties without any clinic frames access as a question of physical proximity and infrastructure rather than service volume. This metric makes the problem appear more structural and harder to dismiss — even if abortions are still occurring, people in counties without clinics must travel. I considered using total abortion counts, but that would have allowed a counter-reading in which services appear to still be happening. The county-level absence measure closes that interpretive gap.

4. Visualization B — Argues against the proposition

This visualization argues that abortion services in the United States are actively used across a wide range of states. Rather than measuring whether a county has a clinic, it shifts the metric entirely to abortion rates — the number of abortions per 1,000 women aged 15–19 by state of residence in 2017. By measuring utilization rather than geographic availability, the chart reframes the question: people are still obtaining abortions at meaningful rates across most of the country, regardless of how many counties have a local clinic.

The chart includes all states and D.C. without filtering, sorted from lowest to highest abortion rate so that the viewer's eye is drawn toward the high end. Color here encodes a genuinely different variable — the percentage of counties without a clinic — so that darker bars indicate states with better clinic access and lighter bars indicate states with fewer clinics. This creates an additional layer of argument: some light-colored bars (poor clinic access) still appear at moderate abortion rates, suggesting that people travel to obtain services even where local infrastructure is limited.

4A. Key design decisions

Decision 1: Switching the metric from clinic presence to abortion rates Score: −1

By measuring abortion rates rather than clinic distribution, the visualization sidesteps the geographic access argument entirely. Even in states where many counties lack a clinic, abortions are still occurring — because people travel to obtain them. This metric conflates accessibility with utilization, which is a rhetorical sleight of hand: a high abortion rate does not mean access is easy, but the chart is designed to make readers interpret it that way. I considered showing provider counts, but abortion rates by state of residence more directly imply that services are being used, which is the stronger counter-argument.

Decision 2: Including all states without filtering Score: 1.5

Unlike Visualization A, this chart includes all 50 states and D.C. with no filtering. This makes the visualization appear more complete and balanced, and it also allows the high-rate outliers — New York, New Jersey, D.C. — to be visible. Showing the full dataset signals transparency while simultaneously letting the most compelling data points remain in view. It would have been more deceptive to filter, so the earnest choice here also serves the persuasive goal.

Decision 3: Color encodes clinic availability rather than the same variable as bar length Score: 1

In this chart, bar length encodes the abortion rate while color encodes a separate variable: the percentage of counties in that state without a known clinic. Darker blue indicates better clinic access; lighter blue indicates fewer clinics. This adds a genuine second dimension to the chart and creates an implicit argument: lighter bars (poor clinic access) still show non-trivial abortion rates, which supports the claim that limited clinic availability does not eliminate abortion access entirely. This is an earnest encoding choice — color and length tell different parts of the story — and it strengthens the persuasive case without fabricating information.

Decision 4: Annotation for Alaska anchored to its actual data value Score: 0.5

Alaska is annotated specifically because it represents an extreme case: it is among the states with the fewest clinics relative to its geography, yet it still records an abortion rate of 6.6 per 1,000 women aged 15–19. The annotation is tied directly to the Alaska bar and names its rate, so the reader can verify the claim against the axis. This is a grounded annotation that makes a specific, checkable argument rather than a vague one. The D.C. annotation is similarly anchored: it calls out the highest rate (19.8) and is placed adjacent to that bar.

Decision 5: Sorting from lowest to highest rate Score: −0.5

Sorting from low to high means the most compelling data points — the states with the highest abortion rates — appear at the bottom of the chart, which is where the reader's eye lands last but also where it rests. This placement gives the high-rate states a visual "punch" at the end of the chart and makes them feel like a conclusion rather than an outlier. An alphabetical or reverse sort would have buried these states or made them seem less central to the argument.

5. Final Reflection

The most striking outcome of this process was how completely the choice of metric determined the argument. Visualization A and Visualization B use data collected by the same organization about the same topic — abortion services in the United States — but produce nearly opposite impressions. Visualization A asks: does your county have a clinic? Visualization B asks: are abortions still happening in your state? These are genuinely different questions, and neither is dishonest on its own terms. The deception lies not in fabricating data but in presenting one question as if it were the only relevant one. Filtering Visualization A to exclude states with better clinic distribution was the decision I felt most uncomfortable with, because it allowed a regional pattern to read as a national one — which crossed the line from emphasis into misrepresentation.

Revising these visualizations also clarified something concrete about color encoding: when color encodes the same variable as bar length, it adds emotional weight but no new information — which is a form of redundancy that a reader might intuitively sense even if they can't articulate it. Giving color a distinct role (region in Vis A, clinic availability in Vis B) makes each chart carry more analytical content while also making the persuasive argument subtler and harder to dismiss. I now define ethical visualization as requiring not just accurate numbers but also visible acknowledgment of what the data cannot show. In this case, neither chart can show a trend over time — the data are snapshots — and the original titles implied change that the data did not support. That gap between what a title implies and what the data can actually demonstrate is, I think, where the sharpest ethical line sits: persuasive framing that a careful reader could recover from is acceptable; framing that systematically prevents recovery crosses into deception.

6. Data Transformations & Notes

  • Visualization A filter: Limited to states where ≥70% of counties lacked a known abortion clinic in 2020 (37 of 50 states + D.C. met this threshold). States below this cutoff — including California, New York, Connecticut, and others — were excluded.
  • Visualization A sort: States sorted descending by percent of counties without a clinic.
  • Visualization A color: Categorical — dark red for Southern states, lighter red for non-Southern states. Sourced from U.S. Census Bureau regional classification.
  • Visualization B: All 50 states and D.C. included, sorted ascending by abortion rate per 1,000 women aged 15–19 (2017 data).
  • Visualization B color: Continuous blue scale encoding the percentage of counties without a known abortion clinic (2020). Darker = better clinic access; lighter = fewer clinics.
  • No derived fields: Both primary variables were taken directly from the Guttmacher dataset without additional calculation.
  • Known limitation: The two visualizations use data from different years (2020 and 2017) because those were the most complete available for each respective measure. This makes direct comparison across visualizations imprecise.

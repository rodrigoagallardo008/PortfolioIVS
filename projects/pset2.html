Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions

Prior research (such as the Homes For All report below) has shown that corporate and absentee landlords evicted more people during COVID in the state of MA. Evictions can have devastating impacts on residents, families and communities. Conversely, live-in (or live-nearby) landlords tended to file for fewer evictions, leading the report to make a recommendation that local control of property contributes to housing stability. Communities of color, especially Black and Latinx people, faced particularly high rates of eviction. Do these findings hold up in the City of Boston? Are there more evictions in census tracts with less owners living in them? How have rates of owner occupancy and corporate ownership in Boston changed over time? Are there more corporate landlords now than there have been in the past?

1. Introduction

Housing stability is shaped not only by rents and income, but also by who owns residential property. Corporate and absentee ownership can change incentives around rent collection, turnover, and long-term investment, which may affect neighborhood stability over time. This report explores neighborhood-level changes in corporate ownership and owner-occupancy across Boston from 2004 to 2024, combining property assessment–derived rates with neighborhood Census fields (population, race/ethnicity counts, and housing unit counts).

The goal of this analysis is exploratory rather than causal. I use visualizations to describe long-run trends, identify spatial concentration and outliers, and test whether ownership measures align with demographic composition and a housing stability proxy (vacancy). These patterns help generate hypotheses and clarify what additional data would be needed to evaluate eviction-related outcomes more directly.

Motivation from the readings

The Eviction Lab’s national record analysis emphasizes that eviction is widespread, spatially concentrated, and linked to broader instability for families and communities (Badger & Bui, 2018). In Massachusetts, Homes for All argues that corporate and absentee landlords filed more evictions during COVID and that communities of color faced disproportionate exposure (Homes for All MA & Huntley, 2022). Because my dataset does not include eviction filings, I focus on ownership structure and neighborhood context as upstream conditions that may relate to eviction risk.

Overall Analysis Questions (with motivation)

  1. How have corporate ownership and owner-occupancy rates changed across Boston neighborhoods from 2004–2024? The MA report highlights corporate landlords as key eviction filers during COVID, so it is important to see whether corporate ownership has been increasing structurally over time. If corporate ownership rises citywide but unevenly, that can help identify where potential exposure may be growing.
  2. Which neighborhoods experienced the largest shifts in ownership structure between 2004 and 2024? National eviction records show that housing instability clusters spatially, not uniformly. Identifying the largest “movers” in corporate ownership helps pinpoint neighborhoods where investor expansion may be most concentrated.
  3. Are neighborhoods with higher shares of Black and Hispanic residents associated with higher corporate ownership or lower owner-occupancy? Both readings emphasize racial disparities in eviction exposure, especially for Black and Latinx communities. This question checks whether Boston’s ownership structure overlaps with neighborhoods that are cultural and demographic hubs, such as Roxbury and East Boston.
  4. Is higher corporate ownership associated with housing instability indicators (e.g., higher vacancy or lower owner-occupancy)? Eviction is one outcome of instability, but not the only signal. Since this dataset lacks eviction filings, vacancy and owner-occupancy serve as descriptive proxies that can flag neighborhoods with higher turnover, speculative holding, or weaker local control.

Data used

  • Corporate ownership & owner-occupancy over time (2004–2024): neighborhood-year rates.
  • Corporate ownership & owner-occupancy with Census fields: neighborhood-year rates merged with neighborhood population and housing unit counts.
  • Neighborhoods & zip codes: a lookup table used for reference/joins when needed.

Important note on measurement: Corporate ownership is inferred via keyword matching in owner names (e.g., “LLC”, “Trust”), which may undercount or misclassify some owners. Owner-occupancy and corporate ownership are expressed as rates (0–1) at the neighborhood-year level.

4. Methods Notes (Data Transformations)

  • Percent demographic shares: computed as group population / total population (e.g., black_all / tot_pop_all; hisp_all / tot_pop_all).
  • Vacancy rate: computed as vacant_unit / tot_unit.
  • Change metrics (2004→2024): computed as 2024 value − 2004 value for corporate ownership and owner-occupancy rates.
  • Binning: some comparisons use terciles/quartiles of demographic shares to summarize differences across neighborhood groups.

5. Summary

From 2004 to 2024, Boston shows a broad shift toward higher corporate ownership alongside a gradual decline in owner-occupancy. While most neighborhoods trend in the same direction, the pace differs substantially, suggesting investor expansion is uneven across the city. This aligns with the readings’ emphasis that housing instability often concentrates in particular places rather than spreading uniformly.

Neighborhoods with the fastest increases tend to be shaped by redevelopment, centrality, or high rental demand (e.g., Downtown, Fenway, South Boston Waterfront). In contrast, outer neighborhoods such as West Roxbury and Hyde Park remain comparatively more owner-occupied and show slower growth in corporate ownership. This suggests that location and market pressure may be key drivers of ownership restructuring.

Demographic context complicates a single equity conclusion. Corporate ownership is not uniformly highest in the neighborhoods with the largest shares of Black residents, but culturally significant hubs such as Roxbury and parts of Dorchester still show meaningful increases over time. East Boston similarly stands out as a Hispanic hub with strong recent growth in corporate ownership, motivating deeper place-based follow-up.

Overall, these results support the readings’ framing that ownership structure matters and that instability concentrates in specific neighborhoods (Badger & Bui, 2018; Homes for All MA & Huntley, 2022). The main limitation is that I cannot measure eviction directly with this dataset. The clearest next step is to join tract-level ownership measures to eviction filings and test whether the ownership–eviction relationship differs in neighborhoods such as Roxbury and East Boston.

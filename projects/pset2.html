Subtheme: Corporate Landlords & Evictions

Prior research (such as the Homes For All report below) has shown that corporate and absentee landlords evicted more people during COVID in the state of MA. Evictions can have devastating impacts on residents, families and communities. Conversely, live-in (or live-nearby) landlords tended to file for fewer evictions, leading the report to make a recommendation that local control of property contributes to housing stability. Communities of color, especially Black and Latinx people, faced particularly high rates of eviction. Do these findings hold up in the City of Boston? Are there more evictions in census tracts with less owners living in them? How have rates of owner occupancy and corporate ownership in Boston changed over time? Are there more corporate landlords now than there have been in the past?

1. Introduction

Housing stability is shaped not only by rents and income, but also by who owns residential property. Corporate and absentee ownership can change the incentives and decision-making structures around housing, potentially affecting neighborhood stability over time. This report explores neighborhood-level changes in corporate ownership and owner-occupancy across Boston from 2004 to 2024, using property assessment–derived rates combined with neighborhood Census fields (population, race/ethnicity counts, and housing unit counts).

The goal of this analysis is exploratory rather than causal. I use visualizations to describe long-run trends, identify spatial concentration and outliers, and test whether ownership measures line up with neighborhood demographic composition and a structural housing indicator (vacancy). These patterns help generate plausible hypotheses and guide what deeper analysis would be needed to evaluate eviction-related questions more directly.

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How have corporate ownership and owner-occupancy rates changed across Boston neighborhoods from 2004–2024?
  2. Are neighborhoods with higher shares of Black and Hispanic residents associated with lower owner-occupancy or higher corporate ownership?
  3. Is higher corporate ownership associated with lower housing stability indicators (e.g., vacancy rate or lower owner-occupancy)?
  4. Do neighborhoods experiencing population growth or housing unit expansion also show faster increases in corporate ownership?

Data used

  • Corporate ownership & owner-occupancy over time (2004–2024): neighborhood-year rates.
  • Corporate ownership & owner-occupancy with Census fields: neighborhood-year rates merged with neighborhood population and housing unit counts.
  • Neighborhoods & zip codes: a lookup table used for reference/joins when needed.

Important note on measurement: Corporate ownership is inferred via keyword matching in owner names (e.g., “LLC”, “Trust”), which may undercount or misclassify some owners. Owner-occupancy and corporate ownership are expressed as rates (0–1) at the neighborhood-year level.

4. Methods Notes (Data Transformations)

  • Percent demographic shares: computed as group population / total population (e.g., black_all / tot_pop_all; hisp_all / tot_pop_all).
  • Vacancy rate: computed as vacant_unit / tot_unit.
  • Change metrics (2004→2024): computed as 2024 value − 2004 value for corporate ownership and owner-occupancy rates.
  • Binning: some comparisons use terciles/quartiles of demographic shares to summarize differences across neighborhood groups.

5. Summary

From 2004 to 2024, Boston shows a broad shift toward higher corporate ownership alongside a gradual decline in owner-occupancy. While most neighborhoods trend in the same direction, the pace differs substantially, suggesting investor expansion is uneven across the city.

Neighborhoods with the fastest increases tend to be shaped by redevelopment, centrality, or high rental demand (e.g., Downtown, Fenway, South Boston Waterfront). In contrast, outer neighborhoods such as West Roxbury and Hyde Park remain comparatively more owner-occupied and show slower growth in investor ownership.

Adding demographic context complicates a simple equity narrative. At the neighborhood scale, corporate ownership is not uniformly highest in neighborhoods with the largest shares of Black residents. However, historically Black communities such as Roxbury and parts of Dorchester still show meaningful increases in corporate ownership over time. Roxbury stands out as both a long-standing cultural hub of Boston’s Black community and a neighborhood where investor ownership rises steadily across the time window.

A parallel case emerges for East Boston, which functions as an immigrant gateway neighborhood and a major hub for Boston’s Hispanic community. East Boston combines a high Hispanic share with strong growth in corporate ownership, suggesting that investor expansion may intersect with culturally significant neighborhoods in locally important ways.

Overall, these patterns motivate a next step: pairing tract-level corporate ownership with eviction filings (if available) to test whether investor ownership is associated with eviction risk and whether that relationship differs for culturally significant neighborhoods such as Roxbury and East Boston.
